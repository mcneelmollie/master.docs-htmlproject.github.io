Greening Up Concrete Jungles

Green roofs are sprouting up across the country and highlighting how sustainability and city living can coexist.

By Mollie McNeel

An aerial view of most urban areas shows patches of asphalt, black tar and gravel-clad rooftops.

Heat waves radiate off the dark roofs, and water rushes over the hard, impermeable surfaces. But there’s a new trend occurring across the United States that breaks up the monotony of these common roofs—green rooftops.

Green rooftops have begun to appeal to homeowners, businesses and even major cities as an attractive way to promote environmentalism, while also solving problems associated with conventional roofs. Green roofs supplement traditional vegetation without disrupting urban infrastructure.

Green roofs transform a neglected space, making it useful and some would say: beautiful.

“Generally, there are three types of green roofs: intensive, semi-intensive and extensive green roofs,” explained Green Project Administrator, Michael Berkshire.

Intensive green roofs:

  • Essentially elevated parks
  • Can sustain shrubs, trees, walkways and benches
  • Have complex structural support, irrigation, drainage and root protection layers
  • Require a foot or more of soil
  • Creates a load of approximately 80-150 pounds per square foot
  • Requires routine maintenance

Semi-intensive green roof:

  • Can sustain small herbaceous plants, ground covers, grasses and small shrubs
  • Requires moderate maintenance and occasional irrigation
  • Requires 6 to 12 inches of soil
  • Creates a load between 25-40 pounds per square foot
  • Provides potential of a traditional garden

Extensive green roofs:

  • relatively light and weigh anywhere from 15-50 pounds per square foot
  • Requires only 2-5 inches of soil
  • Supports native ground cover such as herbs, mosses and grasses
  • Requires little maintenance
  • Exist solely for environmental benefits
  • Don't function as accessible rooftop gardens.
  • Least expensive among the three types of green roofs in terms of installation
  • Has self-retaining capabilities

The idea of a living roof isn’t new. They were common among sod houses on the American prairie and on log houses throughout northern Europe. But recently, architects, builders and city planners all across the planet have begun turning to green roofs: not for their beauty, but for their practicality.

Green roofs have numerous benefits, including improving a building's water management, reducing cooling and heating costs of a building and increasing the lifespan of a building's roof.

When rain falls on a conventional roof, it runs off the buildings roof—unabsorbed, unfiltered and practically undeterred. A green roof works the way a garden does by absorbing water, filtering it, slowing it down, even storing some for later use.

This process helps delay the discharge of rainwater to the sewage system, allows water to evaporate through the plants and ultimately stabilizes the groundwater level, reducing the peak load on the sewage system and the risk of flooding.

“We have a combined sewer system here in Chicago so all of the storm water goes into the same pipes as the sanitary sewage,” said Berkshire.

In the occurrence of heavy rainfalls, the system can become full which causes sewage to back up into people's basements. This causes street flooding and if severe enough, then the lock and dams have to be opened allowing the sewage to flow into Lake Michigan.

During the summer, daytime temperatures on traditional asphalt rooftops can be extremely high, reaching above 150 degrees Fahrenheit and contributing to the overall “urban heat island effect”—the tendency of cities to be warmer than surrounding regions.

Chicago’s City Hall roof is divided into two halves. One of which is for city offices and the other for county, with only the city side being covered in vegetation. Up until a few years ago, the county’s side was standard black asphalt.

Comparison of rooftops on Chicago's City Hall

"We were monitoring the temperature differences on each side," Berkshire says. "On a 90 degree day, the roof on the vegetated side always hovered around 90 degrees. Whereas the county side was as high as 170 degrees fahrenheit."

According to a report conducted by the American Society of Landscape Architects, soil mixture along with a green roofs vegetation act as insulation causing temperatures to fluctuate only mildly, reducing heating and cooling costs in the buildings below them by as much as 20 percent.

Green roofs have a longer life expectancy than traditional roofs and can last at least twice as long without needing to be replaced. Common roof membranes are broken down due to extreme environmental factors such as direct sunlight and fluctuating temperatures. The additional layers provided by a green roof help to mediate extreme temperature differences.

In terms of cost, green infrastructure is frequently 5-30 percent less costly to construct and about 25 percent less costly over its life cycle compared with traditional infrastructure according to a report conducted by the Illinois Environmental Protection Agency.

In 2004, Chicago implemented the Sustainable Development Policy requiring all buildings that receive financial assistance from the City, are part of a planned development, or are located on the Chicago waterway system or near Lake Michigan to have a green roof.

This policy has since been updated with the addition of an advisory committee and restructured according to public feedback.

The revised policy allows development teams to choose from a menu of strategies that can be customized to fit the project’s characteristics.

Each strategy is given a certain point value. New projects are required to achieve at least 100 points, whereas renovations of existing buildings are required to reach 25 or 50 points depending on the scale of the renovation.

This program forged a radical shift throughout Chicago, significantly increasing the city’s overall sustainability. Data from the city shows it’s now home to over 300 green roofs, but Berkshire states that as of 2013, Chicago had more than 500 green roofs totaling nearly 5.6 million square feet.

This is more than any other city in North America and places Chicago in the top five cities globally.

Based on a previous analysis, it is estimating that over 80 percent of the current green roofs were built because of the Sustainable Development Policy.

Chicago is creating green spaces where asphalt and gravel once resided, highlighting how sustainability and city living can coexist, and returning some of that space back to nature.
